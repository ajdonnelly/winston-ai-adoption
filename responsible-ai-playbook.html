Sample deliverable · Responsible-AI enablement playbook

A 90-day responsible-AI adoption pilot

A role-based enablement model for a law firm — shown not as a firmwide push, but as one bounded, governed pilot: a single practice group adopting an approved generative-AI assistant for a high-value workflow, with confidentiality, human oversight, and verification built in from day one.

About this sample

Independent work sample created for this application; set at a fictional practice group and not affiliated with or endorsed by Winston Taylor. It is meant to work within a firm’s own AI governance and approved-tool program — not to replace it — and to show how I’d run enablement on the ground. Tools, timelines, and targets are illustrative.

The setting & the bet

One group, one workflow, guardrails first

The pilot group (fictional)

A realistic but invented setting, so the playbook reads as one concrete engagement.

A commercial-litigation practice group — roughly 8–10 attorneys and associates plus a few paralegals and business professionals — adopting the firm’s approved GenAI assistant for a common, high-value workflow: summarizing long documents and producing verifiable first-draft research memos, always under attorney review.

The bet

Why a bounded, governed pilot beats a firmwide rollout.

Start with one group and one workflow, put the guardrails in before anyone touches client data, prove the value safely, and expand only on evidence. Responsible adoption is the foundation, not an afterthought — in a law firm, trust and confidentiality are the product.

The 90-day arc

Discover → Design → Pilot → Measure → Sustain

Five phases, each with an exit condition. Guardrails are set before any live use begins.

1 Discover & guardrails · Days 1–15

Learn the group’s real workflows; choose the starter use case(s); confirm the approved tools and the confidentiality and data-classification rules that govern them; baseline current effort; and name a partner sponsor and champions.

Exit: pilot charter, selected use cases, a one-page guardrail guide, baseline effort captured, sponsor and champions named.

2 Design & onboard · Days 16–30

Build role-based onboarding pathways and just-in-time guides and FAQs; confirm access and data handling; design the workshop curriculum; and define the escalation routes for questions, suspected errors, or misuse.

Exit: onboarding pathways, quick-reference guides, escalation matrix, and a workshop plan.

3 Pilot · Days 31–60

Run the workshops, go live on the starter use cases with attorney review on everything, hold weekly office hours, and capture vetted use cases and anti-patterns as they emerge. Coach the two habits that protect the firm: verify against source, and never put client data into an unapproved tool.

Exit: the group trained; first AI-assisted work produced and human-reviewed; champions active; a growing use-case and anti-pattern log.

4 Measure & refine · Days 61–75

Move from anecdote to evidence: adoption, time saved, quality, and confidence — plus a review of any risk signals. Refine the guides against what the data shows and prepare an evidence-based expansion recommendation.

Exit: scorecard vs. baseline, refined materials, risk review, and a drafted recommendation.

5 Expand & sustain · Days 76–90

A disciplined expansion decision; a champion network; a 30/60/90 reinforcement plan; and a standing feedback loop to Practice Innovation, Knowledge, Risk, Security, and IT.

Exit: outcomes report, v2 materials, champion guide, reinforcement plan, and a cross-functional feedback loop.

Honest about friction

Where adoption stalls — and how governance holds

What actually derails firm AI adoption

Lawyers won’t rely on output they can’t verify — and some will over-trust it, which is just as risky. People fear getting it wrong, or being blamed for an AI error. Anxiety about confidentiality keeps cautious users away entirely. One hallucination can make someone abandon the tool. Workshop attendance is not behavior change. And use slips once the initial push fades. The answer is the same throughout: clear guardrails, real verification habits, named escalation, and champions who model safe success. I can name these honestly because I’ve sat on the firm side of exactly this work.

The other half of the work

An internal plan needs a public-facing position

A clear internal program is only half the picture. A firm also needs a considered, public-facing stance on AI — and the two should say the same thing. Where the pilot governs what happens inside, the public position governs what the market understands about it.

What a public stance signals

To clients and competitors alike.

A clear position tells the market what the firm is thinking about AI — and, most importantly, what it is doing about it: the services and capabilities it is building to serve clients better. Saying nothing is itself a signal, and rarely the one a firm wants to send. A considered statement turns quiet internal work into a visible commitment.

Meet clients where they are

Different clients, different starting points.

Like the general public, clients hold a wide range of views on AI — and many have already set their own adoption policies, from enthusiastic to restrictive. A good public stance is legible to the cautious and the eager at once: it reassures clients who want strict limits while showing ambitious ones that the firm can move with them, and it aligns with how each client wants AI used on their matters.

Internal and external, in sync

The public statement should be backed by the internal program, never ahead of it — say only what the pilot makes true. Stance and substance move together: the governance, guardrails, and measured results from a pilot like this one are exactly what give a public position its credibility.
