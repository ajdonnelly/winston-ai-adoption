Legal AI adoption is mostly a people problem opportunity.

The models get better every quarter. Adoption doesn’t keep pace. If capability were the constraint, every practice group in the country would already be transformed — the tools available today are powerful, and anyone who has worked with them seriously knows it. But from my experience inside a firm, the obstacle is rarely technical. Legal AI adoption is mostly a people issue — and I’d reframe it as an opportunity, rather than the problem I hear it named so frequently. The work that unlocks adoption is human work, and that is exactly where the leverage is.

I have come to put it in chemistry terms: the limiting reagent in any organization’s AI adoption is emotional and psychological buy-in from its people. A firm can have capability, sponsorship, and budget in abundance, but adoption proceeds only at the rate that buy-in allows. Without it, two things quietly stall: the change in workflows, and the critical feedback loop from the people doing the work. A tool nobody trusts produces no findings — and a pilot that produces no findings was never really a pilot.

Buy-in does not come from a kickoff meeting. It starts with level-setting: bringing every stakeholder — partners, associates, paralegals, secretaries, administrators — to a shared understanding of what these systems are and what they can and cannot do. People cannot meaningfully judge a technology they do not understand, and without that baseline every evaluation that follows measures impressions instead of the tool. One distinction does most of the work: language models generate output; they do not retrieve it from a database of facts. Clarifying that early contextualizes hallucination, omission, and the other errors every legal team rightly worries about.

Then comes the unglamorous part, which I believe is the actual job: going to people’s desks. Working them through small pressure points in their real workflows. Making yourself constantly accessible. Short, recurring learning sessions — real matters, real prompts, colleagues demonstrating to colleagues — are where skeptics become daily users. One launch session does not build fluency; a cadence does. An environment of confidence and momentum has to develop in the culture of a rollout, and that environment gets built one desk at a time.

Trust has to be built deliberately, too. What I have found is that an explicit list of what the AI will never touch does more for attorney confidence than any demo. A standing rule of human review on every output makes the tool safe to try. And honest verdicts build the most trust of all — including the verdict that a task is beyond the technology’s current scope. A team that hears “not yet, and here is how we will know when” trusts the next recommendation far more than a team that only ever hears enthusiasm.

None of this diminishes the capability side — it is necessary. But it is never sufficient, because no matter their position in a firm, every person is an expert in their own space, and adoption ultimately runs through their judgment: what good work product looks like, where the pain lives, and what a tool must get right to matter. The firms that succeed with legal AI will not be the ones that bought the best model. They will be the ones that did the hard, human work.
