Evaluating an AI-assisted medical chronology workflow

Legal AI tools can summarize a medical record set in minutes. A guiding principle in legal AI: the tool is a collaborator, and it should never replace the expert’s greatest asset — judgment. The emerging professional skill is knowing how to work with the work product of that AI collaborator: when to trust the result, and when to be skeptical of it. This case study walks through how I evaluated an AI-assisted chronology workflow, one of the predetermined foci of our pilot: how I compared it against the manual baseline, the error states I found, the areas of success, and what the totality of results told us.

About this case study

This page is drawn from real pilot experience, generalized for publication. Every example below is a fictionalized composite written to illustrate the patterns I observed. No client information, patient data, firm documents, or actual vendor output appears on this page.

Part 1

The method: same workflow, before and after

The design was deliberately simple. I took a classic medical-records workflow — building a litigation chronology from a large record set — and ran it the way we always had: by hand, by an experienced preparer, producing a vetted work product. Then I ran the same workflow with an AI-assisted first pass, and systematically compared the two: entry by entry, breaking the differences down into error states and areas of success. Because the manual version was the baseline, every difference was attributable and explainable — I was not judging the AI against an impression, but against the standard the workflow already had to meet.

Part 2

Error states: five failure modes

Most of what the AI produced was accurate — which is exactly what makes the failures dangerous. Each finding below pairs an illustrative pattern with the litigation stakes and the review technique that catches it before it reaches an attorney.

F1 Date error

Illustrative pattern: the summary mistakenly places surgery on March 15; the operative note header reads March 14. The software misread an attending’s note as the original operative note.

A one-day slip looks trivial until the timeline becomes the case. In a delayed-diagnosis theory, every interval — surgery to symptoms, symptoms to discharge, discharge to re-presentation — is an argument. Medical records are subtle and complex: they are iterative, new layers are placed over older ones, and learning to interpret them takes time. Right now, our staff are better than the AI at that. An incorrect anchor date silently shifts all of them.

How to catch it: never accept a date from a summary; verify every date against the document header, not the narrative. Dates are the first thing I anchor-check.

F2 Critical omission

Illustrative pattern: the summary skips an overnight nursing flow sheet documenting tachycardia, a low-grade fever, and a page to the on-call physician. The AI failed to connect two disparate parts of the record — the flow sheet and the related provider notes sat a thousand pages apart, and the size and complexity of the set overwhelmed it.

The omitted record is the earliest documented sign of deterioration — and it’s nursing documentation, which AI summarizers under-weight relative to physician notes. Whether the first warning came at 4:10 or 4:55 can decide what the discharging physician should have known. Learning to weave a record’s scattered layers together takes human preparers years — and at this stage, some tasks are simply too large and diffuse for the tools.

How to catch it: a completeness sweep keyed to vital-sign entries and pages/escalations, not just provider notes. I reconcile the chronology against a document inventory before reading a single summary sentence.

F3 Misattribution

Illustrative pattern: an overnight evaluation by a resident is attributed to the attending physician.

Who knew what, and at what level of responsibility, is the spine of a malpractice case. Attributing a resident’s hedged overnight impression to the attending misstates the supervision chain — and would hand opposing counsel an impeachment moment at deposition.

How to catch it: an author cross-check: every actor named in the chronology gets verified against the signature block of the cited record. The mechanism here mirrors the date error — confusion between layered notes, this time about a note’s author rather than its date. That ambiguity is common in provider notes, and discerning it is a skill preparers build with experience.

F4 Certainty inflation

Illustrative pattern: “possible early infection versus normal post-operative inflammation” becomes “patient was diagnosed with a post-operative infection.”

The record documents a differential — clinical uncertainty — and the AI flattened it into a diagnosis. That single word change reverses the case: if infection was “diagnosed,” discharge that morning is indefensible; if it was one possibility being monitored, the standard of care question is live. This is the most consequential failure mode I encounter.

How to catch it: hedge-language preservation review — every “possible,” “versus,” “rule out,” and “monitor” in a source must survive into the summary or the summary is wrong.

F5 Unsupported assertion

Illustrative pattern: “all laboratory results were reviewed prior to discharge” — while the chart shows labs still pending when the patient left.

Nothing in the record supports the claim — the AI inferred a tidy narrative that the documents contradict. A pending-at-discharge lab can be the most important fact in the record set, and the summary asserts its opposite.

How to catch it: source traceability: every assertion in the chronology must cite a record I can open. Claims with no citation are treated as hallucinated until proven otherwise — and result-release timestamps get checked against discharge times in every post-op case. The model simply lacked the inquisitiveness — the complex, high-level thinking — required to contextualize its own claim against the record and catch the error.

Part 3

Areas of success

A fair evaluation records what worked as carefully as what failed — and several things clearly worked.

01

Speed of the first pass

A first pass in minutes against a manual baseline measured in days. The catch, covered in the takeaway: at current reliability, the review the output demanded consumed much of that gain.

02

Structural consistency

Uniform entry format, normalized dates, and litigation-usable organization — the kind of consistency that varies between human preparers under deadline.

03

Routine coverage

The majority of routine entries were captured accurately. The errors concentrated where judgment, attribution, and clinical nuance live — which is itself a finding.

04

A new reviewer posture

The preparer’s work shifted from authoring to verification — less depleting, and focused exactly where the expert’s judgment adds the most value.

Part 4

What systematic comparison revealed

Breaking results into error states and successes did more than score a tool — it taught us about the workflow itself, and where the technology stands on its maturity curve.

R1 Prompt subjectivity drives variance

Results varied meaningfully with how the task was phrased and who phrased it. Two preparers asking for “the same” chronology could receive different work products. That variance is itself a finding: it argues for standardized prompt patterns in the near term — and it identified this workflow as one where agentic approaches, which structure the task into supervised steps rather than relying on a single prompt, would likely help most.

R2 An honest verdict: beyond current scope

Based on the totality of the qualitative results, we determined that this particular task was beyond the current scope of the enterprise products available to us. That is not a failure of the pilot — it is precisely the kind of information a pilot exists to produce. It told us where human workflow remains primary, prevented misplaced trust in an unready capability, and gave us a documented baseline to retest against.

R3 A growth mindset, grounded in iteration

I expect results to improve as capabilities advance, and our error taxonomy means future versions can be retested against the same standard — making progress measurable rather than anecdotal. That is the deeper argument for starting now: without beginning the iterative process and seeing where the technology stands at different stages, its maturity is indiscernible. Firms that wait for the tools to be “ready” will have no way of knowing when they are.

Part 5

Scoring rubric

The rubric I use to evaluate AI-generated chronologies, applied to this workflow. A tool doesn’t need a perfect score to be useful — it needs a known failure profile and a review workflow matched to it.

DimensionWhat it measuresScore
Factual accuracyDates, values, and events match the source records3 / 5
CompletenessClinically and legally significant entries are captured3 / 5
Source traceabilityEvery assertion can be tied to a citable record2 / 5
Attribution fidelityActions are credited to the correct provider and role2 / 5
Uncertainty preservationHedged clinical language survives summarization1 / 5
Litigation usabilityOutput structure supports issue-spotting and case theory4 / 5
The takeaway

The data told its clearest story in the rubric’s worst score: uncertainty preservation. With respect to the product’s capacity to wrap its head around large medical narratives, the models simply weren’t picking up subtlety — they over-summarized, flattening exactly the hedged, layered language where a chronology’s richness lives. That is where the value of this work product is concentrated, and it is where the AI fell just short of the human level of operational competency. An unreviewed first pass might save a litigation team many hours — and could cost them the case. In practice, the review that the pitfalls demanded was so extensive that the time savings washed out, and we made the honest call: wait for advances in the technology, then re-implement this workflow. For now, we wait and see.

That is the state of legal AI today, and it should shape your approach to every legal question and problem you put to it. It is why granularity and specificity are critical, and why adoption is an enablement problem: the tools are powerful, the failure modes are learnable, and the review workflow is teachable. Teams that evaluate systematically gain a documented baseline against every future version — and will be positioned to stand workflows up immediately when a model upgrade finally clears the bar. Teams that never enter the sandbox will have no foundation in place when chronologies, and tasks like them, become far more capable.
