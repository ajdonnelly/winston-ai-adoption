About

I have already worked as a firm’s AI-adoption person

Most recently, as a senior litigation paralegal, I served on my firm’s AI committee and helped run an enterprise pilot — integrating tools like Microsoft Copilot and Filevine’s Lois into real legal work, testing them against live tasks, evaluating output for reliability, documenting workflows, and co-leading training for colleagues from staff to partners.

That work made me fluent in the part that matters most in a law firm: responsible use. I closely QC’d AI-generated medical chronologies against the source — concrete practice in human-in-the-loop verification, catching hallucinations, and protecting confidential and privileged data.

For more than a decade before that I ran the daily operations of a boutique plaintiff-side firm end to end, through repeated waves of technology adoption — billing and case-management migrations, digitizing files, and getting busy legal professionals to trust new systems. My graduate training in biomedical visualization is about making complex information understandable; this fall I begin the University of Hawaiʻi Executive J.D. focused on AI law and governance.

The approach

Responsible adoption, by design

Getting a firm to use AI is not a tool rollout — it is behavior change, earned safely. Four principles guide the work, and the last one is non-negotiable in a law firm.

01

Meet the work

Learn how each practice group and business team actually works before recommending where AI fits.

02

Enable every role

Role-based pathways for attorneys, associates, paralegals, and business professionals — because each adopts differently.

03

Guardrails first

Confidentiality, data classification, human-in-the-loop review, and citing sources — built into every workflow, not bolted on.

04

Measure value and risk

Track adoption and time saved — and watch for misuse, bias, and hallucination patterns, escalating risk signals early.

How I’d run it

A bounded responsible-AI adoption pilot

Rather than a firmwide push, I’d start with one practice group and one high-value workflow, prove it safely, and expand on evidence — mapping the real work first, then enabling each role with guardrails built in.

Start from the real workflow

Map how a practice group actually works before configuring anything, so AI lands where it helps and where oversight matters.

  1. Intake and issue framing
  2. Research and analysis
  3. Drafting and review
  4. Knowledge capture and reuse
  5. Client delivery

Enable safely, stage by stage

The work that takes a group from curious to confident — with confidentiality and human review throughout.

01

Vision & guardrails

Leadership alignment, approved-tool scope, and the confidentiality and data-handling rules up front.

02

Workshops & onboarding

Role-based, hands-on sessions: prompt technique, verifying outputs, citing sources, and when to escalate.

03

Champions & comms

Identify champions to share safe success stories and reinforce standards through peer learning.

04

Metrics & risk signals

Adoption, time saved, and quality — plus misuse and hallucination signals routed to the right teams.

The responsible-AI enablement suite

These aren’t five standalone samples — they’re one cohesive system, built the way I’d run enablement on the job. Each piece meets people in a different modality and at a different altitude, so complexity rises only as fast as confidence does.

The blueprint deck is the first engagement: the 40,000-foot view that frames the pilot for participants. From there, specificity sharpens — the interactive module teaches the actual habits, one node in a larger learning interface. The tracker puts a tool in the champion’s hands to log what’s working, and that data aggregates upward to inform firm strategy. The podcast reaches the people who’d rather listen than read. And the playbook is the management-facing 90-day plan, setting out the qualities and results responsible adoption should produce. Different modes, different audiences, one through-line — built to work within a firm’s governance, not to replace it.

Why this work

Why legal AI, and why Winston Taylor

I believe responsible AI adoption is one of the most important ways to serve innovation in law firms right now.

The opportunity is not simply introducing new tools; it is helping lawyers and staff use them to reduce friction, improve quality, and free more time for judgment, advocacy, and client care — without ever putting confidentiality or accuracy at risk. Having been the person inside a firm doing this work, I know it lives or dies on trust: people adopt AI when they understand its limits, can verify its output, and know the guardrails protect them and their clients.

Winston Taylor is building this function deliberately — an AI Adoption Specialist working across practice groups with Practice Innovation, Knowledge, Risk, Security, and IT, under a Director of Legal Technology & Innovation. That is exactly the cross-functional, governed, attorney-centric environment where I do my best work. I would be glad to bring my firm-operations experience, medical-legal verification habits, and hands-on AI enablement to help your people adopt these tools responsibly and well.
