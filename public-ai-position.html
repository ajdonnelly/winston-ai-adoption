Say it out loud: why a firm needs a public AI position.

Most of my work is internal — the guardrails, the training, the patient business of getting people to trust a tool. But I have come to believe a firm’s internal plan is only half the work. The other half faces outward: a considered, public-facing position on AI. Where the pilot governs what happens inside the firm, the public position governs what the market understands about it — and in a profession built on trust, what the market understands is not a side issue.

A clear stance signals two things at once. To competitors, it says where the firm stands and how seriously it is moving. To clients, it says something more important: not just what the firm is thinking about AI, but what it is doing — the services and capabilities it is building to serve them better. Silence sends a signal too, and rarely the one a firm wants. In a moment when every client is forming an opinion about AI in legal work, declining to have a public position doesn’t read as caution; it reads as absence.

The harder truth is that there is no single audience to address. Like the general public, clients hold a wide range of views on AI — and many have already written their own adoption policies, running from enthusiastic to restrictive. One client’s in-house team may be automating intake and expecting their outside counsel to keep pace; another may forbid putting their matter near a generative tool at all. A public position that speaks only to the eager alienates the cautious, and one that speaks only to the cautious looks timid to the rest. The work is to be legible to both: to reassure clients who want strict limits that the firm respects them, while showing ambitious clients that the firm can move with them.

That is why I think the most credible public statement is also the most specific. Vague reassurance — “we take AI and security seriously” — persuades no one, because everyone says it. What lands is the concrete: what the firm uses AI for and what it never will, how client data is protected, where a human always reviews the work, and how the firm decides when a new use is safe to adopt. Those are not marketing claims; they are the same boundaries the internal program already runs on, simply made visible. A position stated at that level of detail invites the client conversation that matters most — how do you want AI used on your matters? — rather than foreclosing it.

The one rule I would never break: the public statement must be backed by the internal program, never ahead of it. Say only what the pilot makes true. A firm that advertises capabilities it cannot yet deliver responsibly is making a confidentiality and competence promise it may not keep — the most expensive kind of overstatement in this profession. Stance and substance have to move together, and they reinforce each other when they do: the governance, guardrails, and measured results from a disciplined pilot are exactly what give a public position its credibility, and a public position is what turns that quiet internal work into something clients can actually see and trust.
