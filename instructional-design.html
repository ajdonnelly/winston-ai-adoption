What firm AI adoption can learn from instructional design.

Throughout my career, I have been drawn to technologies that expand human capability and improve the communication of complex information. My graduate training is in biomedical visualization — a field whose entire purpose is making complex information learnable by a specific audience. My thesis research asked how jurors comprehend expert medical testimony, and whether haptic, self-guided, three-dimensional printed models could improve that comprehension while reducing cognitive burden. I mention this because, after helping bring about AI adoption inside a law firm, I am convinced that firm AI adoption is, at its core, a problem my field has studied for decades: adoption is a learning problem, and learning problems have a design discipline.

Instructional design starts where most rollouts skip: audience analysis. A juror, a surgeon, and an attorney need different things from the same anatomical model — and a partner, an associate, a paralegal, and a legal secretary need different things from the same AI tool. They hold different prior knowledge, different anxieties, and different workflows. Enablement built for “the user” reaches none of them; enablement built role by role, the way a good lesson is built for its learners, reaches all of them. This is why fact-finding by role is the first thing I do in any adoption effort.

The second lesson is cognitive load. The instinct of every rollout is the comprehensive manual, the hour-long webinar, the everything-launch. Learning theory says the opposite: people acquire skills in small, task-specific units, anchored to work they already do, with immediate opportunity to practice. A two-page quick-reference guide for one real workflow outperforms a hundred-page manual every time. The same principle holds for the tools themselves — every additional choice a user must make before getting value is load that an anxious learner will resolve by closing the tab.

Third: learning is social and spaced, not broadcast and singular. Large organizations learn the way individuals do — through repetition, scaffolding, and watching peers. Short recurring sessions where colleagues demonstrate to colleagues build more fluency than any kickoff. And assessment runs both directions: what learners get wrong tells the teacher what is not landing. Inside a firm, I believe user struggles are not complaints to manage — they are the most precise signal we have about what the rollout and the guardrails must fix, if someone is designed into the loop to capture them.

Finally, durable knowledge transfer. Tool updates are a curriculum problem disguised as a release-notes problem: a feature announcement tells users what changed; instruction tells them what it means for their Tuesday. The adoption programs that translate every change into workflow impact — and that help a firm build an internal knowledge base where one person’s discovery becomes everyone’s practice — are doing instructional design whether they call it that or not. I’ve built this kind of material in Articulate Rise and coordinated curriculum for a medical scholars program, and the principles transfer exactly.

Firms are evaluating AI on model capability today. I believe they will win or lose on learning velocity, because a firm can only adopt as fast as its people learn. The firms that staff and design for that — the way educators would — will out-adopt the ones that buy licenses, send release notes, and hope for the best.
