The downward force: clients are shaping legal AI adoption.

Most conversations about AI adoption in law firms are framed as internal change management: how to get attorneys comfortable, how to govern the tools, how to train staff. All of that matters — I spend most of my time on exactly those problems. But the strongest force I have observed acting on legal AI adoption doesn’t come from inside the firm at all. It comes down from above, from clients.

What has been really interesting is watching one of our institutional clients begin using AI on a large, complex, multiparty matter I supported. It changed the texture of the engagement: the pace of communication, the form in which information moved, the expectations about what counsel could absorb and how quickly. In-house legal teams and corporate clients — healthcare organizations conspicuously among them — are not waiting for their law firms to modernize. They are adopting AI inside their own operations, and their expectations flow downhill to everyone who serves them.

In medical-legal litigation, that downward force has a particular shape. Client communication in this sector follows established norms. The data being shared is among the most sensitive that exists — protected health information moving between health systems, insurers, and counsel. And the substantive work leans heavily on two research domains at once: medical literature and case law. Each of those realities constrains and directs how AI can responsibly enter the workflow. In my experience helping shape an enterprise AI pilot, client realities like these drove many of the most consequential choices — not because the client was in the room, but because the firm’s obligations to them were.

That, to me, is the real lesson: AI adoption is a team sport. It has been enlightening to see what that actually looks like — not a firm buying a tool, or a vendor shipping features, but collaborative, high-level problem solving among the firm, the vendor, and the gravity of client needs, iterated until the tool fits the practice rather than the other way around. The most stimulating working sessions I have been part of were exactly that: the people who do the work, the people who build the product, and the requirements of the clients being served, all in one conversation.

I have no doubt the downward force will intensify, and quickly. Early-adopting clients are building increasingly AI-augmented systems of their own. As their intake, records, compliance, and communication infrastructure becomes AI-native, their outside counsel will be expected to interoperate — to receive what those systems produce, to answer at the speed those systems enable, and to handle data with the rigor those systems assume. Firms that treat AI adoption as an internal efficiency project will find themselves answering to clients who have already moved.

The practical implication is simple to state and demanding to execute: keep clients close in mind throughout the adoption process. Build use cases from the matters actually served, not from demo data. Let the sector’s communication norms and data-sensitivity requirements shape tool selection and governance from day one. And where the relationship allows, bring client feedback directly into the loop — because the standard your adoption ultimately has to meet is theirs.
